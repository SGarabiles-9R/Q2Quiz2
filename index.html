Jose Rizal José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda (1861–1896) was born in Calamba, Laguna, to Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso. He was a physician, writer, and artist who traveled extensively in Europe and Asia. Rizal came from a well-off family of farmers. Published Noli Me Tangere in 1887; exiled to Dapitan in 1892; executed on December 30, 1896. Authored Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, novels that exposed the abuses of Spanish rule. Advocated peaceful reform and became a symbol of Filipino nationalism.
Andres Bonifacio Andrés Bonifacio y de Castro (1863–1897) was born in Tondo, Manila, to Santiago Bonifacio and Catalina de Castro. He worked as a warehouse clerk and was self-educated, with a passion for reading. Bonifacio was the eldest among his siblings. Founded the Katipunan in 1892; led the Cry of Balintawak in 1896; executed in May 1897. Established the revolutionary society that initiated the Philippine Revolution. Known as the "Father of the Philippine Revolution" for his leadership in the struggle for independence.
Heneral Luna Antonio Narciso Luna de San Pedro y Novicio Ancheta (1866–1899) was born in Binondo, Manila, to Joaquín Luna and Laureana Novicio. He studied pharmacy in the Philippines and Spain and worked as a chemist before becoming involved in military service. Appointed Commander-in-Chief of the Philippine Army in 1899; assassinated on June 5, 1899. Organized a disciplined military force and implemented strategies against American forces. Revered for his military brilliance and uncompromising dedication to the Filipino cause.
Apolinario Mabini Apolinario Mabini y Maranan (1864–1903) was born in Tanauan, Batangas, to Inocencio Mabini and Dionisia Maranan. He studied law and theology at the University of Santo Tomas and later worked as a lawyer. Mabini came from a humble peasant family. Became the first Prime Minister of the Philippines in 1899; exiled to Guam in 1901; returned to the Philippines in 1903. Authored the True Decalogue and provided the framework for the revolutionary government. Known as the "Brains of the Revolution" for his role in shaping the ideals and structure of the Philippine independence movement.
Marcelo H Del Pilar Marcelo H. del Pilar y Gatmaitán (1850–1896) was born in Bulacan, Bulacan, to Julián H. del Pilar and Blasa Gatmaitán. He studied law at the University of Santo Tomas and worked as a lawyer and journalist. Del Pilar was known for his deep roots in his hometown. Became editor of La Solidaridad in 1889; fled to Spain in 1888 to avoid persecution; died in 1896 in Barcelona. Advocated for reforms and fought against Spanish abuses through his writings in La Solidaridad. Played a key role in uniting Filipino reformists abroad and amplifying their voice in the Propaganda Movement.
